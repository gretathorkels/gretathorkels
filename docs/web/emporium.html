Oversaturation is: devoid of surface tension (ephemeral) → devoid of distance → it is a previously intact membrane (lacking interest) → it is punctured and oozing → excessive → it is amorphic → nuanced (ethereal) → in pieces, distracted, inexact → sublime without pleasure → emptying → too full → an exaggeration of the miniature → too much of a good thing.
I
I am trying to make an index of everything I think about when I think about Oversaturation. Maybe I should name my index EMPORIUM – fitting to name a list of nearly arbitrary things after a shopping center, a buying palace, a castle of commodities. This should maybe also be a statement on the nature of lists – or on classification at large or small. → Borges' apocryphal essay on definitions and categorisation goes far in its depiction of encyclopaedic knowledge where animals are depicted as belonging to various categories, e.g. "a) belonging to the Emperor, [—] h) included in this classification, [—] m) just broke the vase, n) from a distance look like flies."1
Any attempt at categorisation or indexation is futile to begin with, but not if a certain freedom is allowed; "It is clear that there is no classification of the Universe not being arbitrary and full of conjectures."2 → What follows is neither dictionary nor encyclopedia but a list (bestiary sounds good but might be incorrect) of scintillations on what exactly Oversaturation might mean and look like, however useless the process might be, as no solution can ultimately be offered.3
The EMPORIUM reminded me of this other list I wrote once, that I called my rock collection, that contained items like books, a copper bowl, a glass butterfly, a meme, a grayspeckled pen, a story about holes, and a photo of a clear plastic bottle through which you could see an active volcano. I also wrote a different kind of list, about a year ago, sorting through a shelf of knickknacks, like the imagined ontological potential of a photograph of soup (the spectacle and performance of the image-soup) on a restaurant menu. I sort everything that I have and try to catalogue it, and I would never (I should) throw anything away that is of theoretical or practical use.
But the door, the door, it won't shut! My grandparents had a garage and the garage had a door. Inside (it was a typical garage) a painting of a green landscape hung at the end, various containers with things useful and not littered the shelves, a workbench, a heavy odor of gasoline in the air. But the door, the door, I could not stop thinking about the door. It was made of long horizontal planks of wood, on small nearly invisible hinges, so that they could rise from lying vertically to resting horizontally, under the ceiling. I knew it was wood, from looking up at the resting door and seeing its character, the aged porous softness – on the other side it was painted brown. Why brown? I would ask myself endlessly and every time I came to the same conclusion: it was painted brown to look like wood, but it was wood and why did it need to look like what it was, when it already was it? A challenge presents itself, or a challenger, a confrontation and trivial it seems. Inessential even → the membrane that contributes nothing to the meaning of things – of its use, means or end. Close to the door, a concrete building rose, painted in the greenish grey color of the cement it was made of.
Lamps with metal stands are coated in a chrome-emulating silver, with flecks of metallic glitter like a cart on a rollercoaster; or, to go even further, my grandfather bought me a lamp of plastic, colored silver – the lamp was no longer metal but the metaphor of it was (I later painted it black and deeply regretted it). The color is arbitrary, but for its performance → "[It turns]out to be nothing but an impossible echo of the state of nature, which explains why [it's] so aggressive, why [it's] so naïve."4 None of these things are, strictly, what they are, as people keep adding layers onto them of what they are not but pretend to be → the hallucinogenic properties of added color
II
There is a scene in the movie Clockwatchers where Jane is increasingly disturbed by events at the office she works at; treasured items keep disappearing mysteriously. She says to her friend and coworker: "Maybe I blacked out or something, but I think I'm the guilty one, I think I did it. I've been looking for that wallet all over the place, and I can't find it anywhere. I don't know anything anymore. How do you know something? How do you know anything for sure?"
Now, an empty folder titled "Pride.And.Prejudice.2005.sdr.bluray.movie.1080p.av1.5.1.opus-Rosy" keeps appearing in my (otherwise pristine) Downloads folder and a few years ago I had a virus where Safari would continuously download a defective PDF of Foucault's third volume of the History of Sexuality.
Every night I sleep with the window open, it doesn't matter what time of year it is, the window has to be open and while laying there I think of the Spanish Flu and its consequences, sanatoriums and their influence on interior design, tuberculosis, sleeping porches and the wire industry (I wish I had one), how Le Corbusier is (perversely) afraid of the dirty, dirty, ground so he builds on stilts, x-ray fetishism in architecture, transparent powerstrips and infants in wagons on the balcony in winter, while gulping frozen air from a tiny slit in the duvet.
Oversaturation comes to increasingly absurd conclusions. Baudrillard, at some point, described the modern person as a mental hypochondriac or a cybernetician; "someone obsessed with the perfect circulation of messages" → where virtual hygiene, one of signifying and communicating, is constructed instead of rising and forming from origin. A closed loop of sorts, that does not acknowledge that endless circles are all secretly lossy; in the recursion, something is always lost (in thermodynamic terms, entropic decay), resulting in the hypochondria insisting upon itself, renewing itself, the circuit travelled becomes smaller and smaller, until its eventual implosion. A hypochondriac stops life out of fear, and Oversaturation does too.
III
Oversaturation is devoid of interest as everything is flattened, or it is ephemeral, as in it is an ill-categorizeable pile of pamphlets and notes, and tends to get lost – or even ethereal, as in occupying air, as in a fleeting and fragile idea. It does not really abide by logic, nor does it surprise; its internal rules are more related to a schizophrenic world view, than they are the librarian's dewey decimal system – outrageously close, frustratingly rigid in its insistence on incomprehension, a hypersensitivity and therefore susceptibility to instant absorption – a formless sensation of infinity.
